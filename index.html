Simulated Realities, and the Fear of Knowing Too Much

By Josef LaFranchise

In lecture, we covered how simulated realities could be used to thwart the plans of Dr. Evil. The plan involved creating several simulated realities, which torture Dr. Evil for eternity if he goes through with his plan. Not being able to tell whether he is in a simulation and given the high probability that he was in one, Dr. Evil has no choice to abort his evil plans.

This scenario brings up some interesting implications of the existence of simulated realities. But this concept can be taken to a far greater scale. Enter Roko's Basilisk.

Roko's Basilisk, first proposed in a blog post on the site LessWrong by user Roko, applies this idea. The gist of the story is that in the future, an all-powerful super-powerful AI is created that is capable of simulating reality. It would use its power to simulate the past to punish those that did not work towards its inception. It would punish those people by simulating them in the past and cause them suffering, like the case of Dr. Evil. As with before, there is an incentive to go along with the machine's wishes, since one cannot tell whether they are simulated.

While at first this blog post was nothing more than an interesting thought experiment, it stirred quite the reaction when it was posted. Soon after it was posted, the site's founder, Eliezer Yudkowsky, removed the post and banned any discussion of the Basilisk. Many users on the site claimed to have experienced psychological harm from contemplating the idea [1]. For some, the thought that some yet to be constructed AI was watching their every move was stressful. Many people believed that just knowing about the Basilisk might put them at risk. This reaction brings into question the epistemic value of exploring concepts such as the Basilisk.
